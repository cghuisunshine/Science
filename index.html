Science 10 — Semester Tutorial (UHill)

Grade 10BC-aligned Big Ideas + Inquiry + Lab Skills
Use as: teacher script • student reading • slide source • unit planner

Overarching Course Goals (Tell Students)

Say to students:
Science is not about memorizing facts. Science is about asking questions, testing ideas, and using evidence to explain the world.

By the end of Science 10, students should be able to:

  • Explain scientific ideas using evidence (not just opinions).
  • Design and interpret experiments using variables and fair-testing principles.
  • Understand how matter and energy interact across biology, chemistry, physics, and space science.
  • Describe how living things and the universe change over time based on scientific explanations.
  • Communicate scientific thinking clearly (writing, speaking, graphs, diagrams).
  • Work safely and collaboratively (lab culture, responsibility, reflection).

Core Competencies Integrated All Semester

Communication
  • Lab reports, claim-evidence-reasoning (CER)
  • Diagrams, graphs, explanations in your own words
  • Group discussion and presentation skills
Thinking
  • Patterns, trends, and cause-effect reasoning
  • Interpreting data and judging evidence quality
  • Designing investigations and revising ideas
Personal & Social Skills
  • Lab safety, shared responsibility, respectful teamwork
  • Self-assessment: strengths, next steps, growth mindset
  • Ethics and impact: choices in science affect people and environment

Part 0 — How to Use This Document

This tutorial is written so you can teach directly from it.

  • Bold = key ideas to emphasize
  • Italic = student-friendly phrasing
  • Check for understanding = quick formative prompts
  • Misconceptions = common mistakes to pre-correct
Recommended lesson rhythm (75–80 min block)
  1. Launch (5–8 min): hook question or demo
  2. Mini-lesson (15–20 min): key concept + examples
  3. Guided practice (15–20 min): small tasks, circulate
  4. Lab / application (20–30 min): collect evidence/data
  5. Closure (5–10 min): CER exit ticket, reflection

Lesson 1.1 — What is Science?

Teacher framing

Say: Science is not about being right the first time. Science is about testing ideas, making mistakes, and improving explanations using evidence.

Core definition

Science is a systematic way of understanding the natural world through observation, measurement, experimentation, and reasoning.

ScienceNot Science
Testable and measurableNot testable (belief/faith-only claims)
Evidence-basedOpinion or authority-based
Revisable with new dataFixed, not open to revision

Activity

Show an image of a phenomenon (e.g., plants leaning toward light). Students write:

  • 5 observations (what they see)
  • 3 inferences (what they think it means)
Misconception to address: “Science proves things.”
Correction: Science supports explanations with evidence; conclusions can change with new data.

Check for understanding

  • What makes a claim scientific?
  • Can scientific ideas change? Why?
Lesson 1.2 — Observations, Inferences, and Bias

Teach explicitly

  • Observation: what your senses/tools detect
  • Inference: an explanation based on observations

Example

  • Observation: The beaker feels warm.
  • Inference: A reaction released energy.

Bias

Bias can affect what we notice and how we interpret results. Scientists reduce bias by using:

  • repeat trials
  • clear measurement methods
  • peer review and transparency

Check for understanding

Ask: Can two people make the same observation but different inferences? (Yes.)

Lesson 1.3 — Scientific Questions

What makes a strong scientific question?

  • Testable with a procedure
  • Has measurable variables
  • Specific (not vague or opinion-based)
Weak QuestionStrong Question
Is sugar bad?How does sugar concentration affect yeast growth?
Is solar power good?How does panel angle affect energy output in sunlight?

Student task

Convert 3 weak questions into strong, testable ones.

Lesson 1.4 — Hypotheses

Hypothesis structure

If (independent variable changes), then (dependent variable changes), because (scientific reason).

Example + breakdown

Example: If temperature increases, then reaction rate increases, because particles move faster and collide more often.

  • Prediction: what will happen
  • Reason: why it happens (science explanation)

Check for understanding

Ask students to underline the IV, DV, and the “because” reasoning in a sample hypothesis.

Lesson 1.5 — Variables & Fair Tests

Core idea

In a fair test, you change only one independent variable at a time so you can trust the conclusion.

Worked example

Question: How does ramp height affect toy car speed?

  • IV: ramp height
  • DV: speed (or time to travel a distance)
  • Controls: same car, same ramp surface, same distance, same release method
Common student error: changing multiple things (height + car + surface).
Fix: if you change more than one thing, you can’t tell what caused the result.

Check for understanding

Give 3 scenarios; students identify IV/DV/controls.

Lesson 1.6 — Measurement & Error

Key ideas

  • No measurement is perfect; all data includes uncertainty.
  • Accuracy: closeness to true value
  • Precision: consistency/repeatability

Dartboard model (quick demo)

  • Accurate + precise: tight cluster at bullseye
  • Precise but not accurate: tight cluster off-center
  • Accurate but not precise: scattered around bullseye

Check for understanding

Ask: “Which matters more in science?” (Often both; depends on purpose.)

Lesson 1.7 — Graphing & Trends

Graphing rules (non-negotiable)

  • X-axis = independent variable
  • Y-axis = dependent variable
  • Include units, clear scale, accurate plotting

Trend interpretation prompts

  • Describe the relationship (increases, decreases, no change).
  • Is it linear or curved?
  • Are there anomalies? What might explain them?
  • What does this trend suggest about cause-effect?
Lesson 1.8 — Lab Safety & Responsibility

Culture-setting message

Say: Safety is not a rule you follow for the teacher. Safety is a responsibility you carry for your group.

Key safety habits

  • Goggles when instructed; hair tied; no food/drink
  • Read procedures before starting
  • Report spills and breakage immediately
  • Clean and reset station

Exit ticket

“What is one safety habit you will practice consistently?”

Lesson 2.1 — What Is Life?

Teach core characteristics

Living things typically:

  • are made of cells
  • use energy
  • grow and reproduce
  • respond to the environment

Check for understanding

Ask: “Is a virus alive?” Use this to introduce how definitions can be debated and refined.

Lesson 2.2 — Cells

Core concept

A cell is the smallest unit of life capable of carrying out life processes.

ProkaryoticEukaryotic
No nucleus (bacteria)Nucleus present (plants, animals)
Simpler structureMore complex organelles

Analogy: cell as factory

Cell PartFactory Role
NucleusControl center (instructions)
MitochondriaPower plant (energy release)
Cell membraneSecurity gate (controls entry/exit)

Check for understanding

“If the mitochondria stop working, what happens to the cell?”

Lesson 2.3 — DNA & Genetic Information

Key statement

DNA does not directly make traits. DNA contains instructions to make proteins, and proteins affect traits.

Student-friendly explanation

DNA is like a set of instructions. Genes are small sections of DNA, like specific recipes. When the cell follows those recipes, it builds proteins that affect how your body looks and works.

Check for understanding

“What is a gene?” “What do proteins do?”

Lesson 2.4 — Genes & Traits (Gene–Environment Interaction)

Teach this clearly

Genes provide potential, and environment can affect how traits appear (gene expression).

Examples

  • Height: genes + nutrition/sleep/health
  • Skin tone: genes + sunlight exposure
  • Athletic performance: genes + training

Check for understanding

Ask: “Give one trait mostly genetic and one trait strongly influenced by environment.”

Lesson 2.5 — Mitosis (Growth & Repair)

Purpose

  • Growth
  • Repair
  • Replacement of damaged cells

Conceptual focus

Mitosis produces genetically identical daughter cells.

Stages explained (focus on what + why)
  • Prophase: chromosomes condense (easier to move)
  • Metaphase: line up in middle (organized split)
  • Anaphase: pulled apart (separating copies)
  • Telophase: nuclei reform (two new control centers)

Check for understanding

“Why does the body need identical cells for growth?”

Lesson 2.6 — Meiosis (Variation & Reproduction)

Purpose

Meiosis produces sex cells (gametes) and creates genetic variation.

FeatureMitosisMeiosis
PurposeGrowth/repairReproduction
# of cells made24
Genetic makeupIdenticalDifferent
Key idea: Genetic variation increases the chance that some individuals survive if the environment changes.

Check for understanding

“Why is variation helpful for a species?”

Lesson 2.7 — Natural Selection

Mechanism (teach as 4 steps)

  1. Variation exists in a population.
  2. More offspring are produced than can survive (overproduction).
  3. There is competition for resources.
  4. Individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more (differential survival).
Say: Individuals don’t evolve — populations do, across generations.

Check for understanding

“If one rabbit becomes faster during its lifetime, does that mean the species evolved?” (No — evolution is population-level over generations.)

Lesson 2.8 — Biodiversity & Ethics

Connect science to real life

  • Habitat loss and conservation
  • Human impact on ecosystems
  • Ethics of genetic testing / engineering (age-appropriate)

Discussion prompt

“Should humans intervene to save endangered species? What evidence matters?”

Lesson 3.1 — Matter & Atoms

Core concept

Atoms are the building blocks of matter. Their structure explains chemical behavior.

ParticleChargeMain role
Proton+Identity (element)
Neutron0Stability (mass)
Electron−Bonding (reactivity)

Check for understanding

“If an atom gains an electron, what happens to its charge?” (Becomes negative ion.)

Lesson 3.2 — Periodic Table Logic

Teach patterns (not memorization)

  • Columns (groups) often have similar chemical properties.
  • Valence electrons strongly influence bonding and reactivity.
  • Metals vs non-metals behave differently in bonding and conductivity.

Check for understanding

“Why are elements in the same group similar?”

Lesson 3.3 — Chemical Bonding

Conceptual focus

Atoms bond to become more stable (often by achieving a more stable electron arrangement).

Bond TypeWhat happens?Example
IonicElectron transferNaCl
CovalentElectron sharingH2O

Check for understanding

“Why would sodium transfer an electron to chlorine?”

Lesson 3.4 — Chemical Reactions & Conservation

Core law

Atoms are rearranged in reactions. They are not created or destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions.

Student-friendly meaning

Reactants are the starting substances. Products are what you end up with. The atoms move around to make new combinations.

Check for understanding

“In a reaction, where do the atoms in the products come from?” (From reactants.)

Lesson 3.5 — Energy in Reactions

Energy flow

  • Exothermic: energy leaves the system (often warms surroundings)
  • Endothermic: energy enters the system (often cools surroundings)

Check for understanding

“If a reaction makes the container feel cold, what type might it be?” (Endothermic.)

Lesson 3.6 — Reaction Rates

Key factors + mechanisms

  • Temperature: particles move faster → more collisions
  • Concentration: more particles → more collisions
  • Surface area: more exposed particles → more collisions

Check for understanding

“Why does crushed tablet dissolve faster than a whole tablet?”

Unit 4 — Physics: Energy & Environment (Weeks 11–14)

Big Idea: Energy is conserved, and its transformation can affect the environment.

Lesson 4.1 — What Is Energy?

Concept

Energy is the ability to cause change (motion, heating, sound, light, chemical change).

Check for understanding

“Name one example of energy causing change in your daily life.”

Lesson 4.2 — Forms of Energy (Everyday Anchors)
  • Kinetic: moving objects (running, moving car)
  • Potential: stored energy (object on a shelf)
  • Thermal: heat energy (hot tea, friction)
  • Electrical: moving charges (circuits)
  • Chemical: energy stored in bonds (food, fuel)

Check for understanding

“In a phone battery, what form of energy is stored?” (Chemical.)

Lesson 4.3 — Conservation of Energy

Core law

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transferred or transformed.

Student-friendly explanation

The total energy stays the same, but it can change forms (like from electrical to light and heat).

Check for understanding

“If energy is ‘lost’ as heat, is it actually gone?” (No—still energy, just less useful.)

Lesson 4.4 — Energy Transformations & Efficiency

Real systems

  • Light bulb: electrical → light + thermal (heat)
  • Car engine: chemical (fuel) → kinetic + thermal + sound
  • Wind turbine: kinetic (wind) → electrical
Key idea: In most real systems, some energy becomes thermal energy in the surroundings. This is why systems are not 100% efficient.

Check for understanding

“Why do machines get hot when they run?”

Lesson 4.5 — Energy & Environment

Connect to society

  • Fossil fuels provide lots of energy but can increase greenhouse gases.
  • Renewables reduce emissions but have limitations (location, storage, cost).
  • Efficiency and conservation are often the fastest ways to reduce impact.

Discussion prompt

“What energy choices should our community prioritize, and why?”

Lesson 5.1 — Scale and Structure of the Universe

Hierarchy

Planet → Star → Solar system → Galaxy → Universe

Teaching move

Have students rank objects by size (Earth, Sun, solar system, Milky Way, universe) and explain their reasoning.

Lesson 5.2 — Big Bang Theory (Evidence-Based)

Core idea

The Big Bang Theory explains that the universe began in a very hot, dense state and has been expanding over time.

Evidence (teach conceptually, not just names)

  • Red shift: light from distant galaxies shifts, indicating they are moving away → expansion.
  • Cosmic background radiation: leftover “afterglow” energy from the early universe.

Check for understanding

“If the universe is expanding, what should happen to distances between galaxies over time?” (Increase.)

Lesson 5.3 — Life Cycle of Stars (Connection to Chemistry)

Big connection statement

Many elements in your body were formed in stars. Star life cycles connect space science to chemistry.

Basic life cycle (Grade 10 level)

  • Nebula (gas/dust cloud)
  • Star forms → main sequence stage
  • Star changes as fuel runs out
  • Massive stars can explode as supernovae, forming heavier elements

Check for understanding

“Why is a supernova important for forming heavier elements?” (It creates extreme conditions.)

Simple CER (Claim–Evidence–Reasoning) template for students 
CLAIM:
(My answer to the question.)

EVIDENCE:
(Data, observations, or facts from our work.)

REASONING:
(Why the evidence supports the claim using science ideas.)
