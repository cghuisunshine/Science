Class #7 — Heat and Specific Heat Capacity (C)

7.1 Specific heat capacity

  • Specific heat capacity (C) is the amount of energy required to increase the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K).
  • The coefficient C is constant for specific substances; values have been established through experimentation.
  • Specific heat capacity of water: approximately 4180 J/kg·°C (or 4.18 J/g·°C) (not necessary to memorize)

7.2 Heat (Q) in terms of specific heat capacity (C)

The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of a specific material is:

Q = m C ΔT

Symbols & units

  • Q = heat energy (J)
  • m = mass
    • use grams if C is in J/g·°C
    • use kilograms if C is in J/kg·°C
  • C = specific heat capacity (J/g·°C or J/kg·°C)
  • ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial (°C or K)
Concept: If the same heat Q is added, the substance with lower C has a larger temperature increase.

Extracted Questions

  1. The temperature of 335 g of water changed from 24.5°C to 26.4°C. How much heat did this sample absorb?
  2. How much heat has to be removed from 0.225 kg of water to lower its temperature from 25°C to 10°C?
  3. 1 kg of water at 25°C absorbs 309 kJ of thermal energy from a 2 kW heating element.
    1. What is its final temperature?
    2. How much electrical energy does the heating element consume if it operates at an overall efficiency of 82%?
    3. How long does it take for the heating element to heat the water?

Solutions

1) 335 g water: 24.5°C → 26.4°C

Use: Q = m C ΔT m = 335 g C(water) = 4.18 J/g·°C ΔT = 26.4 − 24.5 = 1.9 °C Q = (335)(4.18)(1.9) Q ≈ 2660 J
Answer: Q ≈ 2.66 × 103 J (absorbed)

2) 0.225 kg water: 25°C → 10°C

Use: Q = m C ΔT m = 0.225 kg C(water) = 4180 J/kg·°C ΔT = 10 − 25 = −15 °C Q = (0.225)(4180)(−15) Q ≈ −14,100 J
Answer: Q ≈ −1.41 × 104 J (heat removed)

3) 1 kg water at 25°C absorbs 309 kJ from a 2 kW heater

(a) Final temperature

Q = 309 kJ = 309,000 J m = 1 kg C(water) = 4180 J/kg·°C ΔT = Q/(mC) = 309,000 / (1×4180) ≈ 74.0 °C Tfinal = 25 + 74.0 ≈ 99.0 °C
Answer: Tfinal ≈ 99°C

(b) Electrical energy consumed (82% efficient)

Efficiency = (useful energy)/(input energy) Input energy: E_in = Q / 0.82 E_in = 309,000 / 0.82 ≈ 3.77 × 10^5 J
Answer: E ≈ 3.77 × 105 J

(c) Time required (2 kW = 2000 W)

Power P = E_in / t → t = E_in / P t = (3.77 × 10^5) / 2000 t ≈ 189 s ≈ 3.15 min
Answer: t ≈ 189 s (≈ 3.1 min)

Other Problems & Solutions (4–9)

Q4 — Silicon

Question: What is the specific heat of silicon if it takes 192 J to raise the temperature of 45 g of Si by 6°C?

C = Q / (m ΔT) C = 192 / (45 × 6) C = 0.711 J/g·°C
Answer: C ≈ 0.711 J/g·°C

Q6 — Lead

Question: What is the specific heat of lead if 96 J raises 75 g by 10°C?

C = Q / (m ΔT) C = 96 / (75 × 10) C = 0.128 J/g·°C
Answer: C ≈ 0.128 J/g·°C

Q5 — Coca-Cola (assume water)

Question: Assuming Coca-Cola has the same specific heat as water, find heat given off when 340 g cools from 25°C to 3°C.

ΔT = 3 − 25 = −22 °C Q = m C ΔT Q = (340 g)(4.18 J/g·°C)(−22 °C) Q = −31,300 J
Answer: 31.3 kJ released (Q is negative because heat leaves the soda)

Q7 — Metal in water (calorimetry)

Question: 75 g water at 24°C + 26 g metal at 82.25°C. Final temp 28.34°C. Find metal’s specific heat.

Heat gained by water = heat lost by metal Qwater = (75)(4.18)(28.34 − 24) = 1360 J Qmetal = (26)(c)(82.25 − 28.34) = (26)(c)(53.91) = 1402.7 c 1360 = 1402.7 c c = 0.97 J/g·°C
Answer: c ≈ 0.97 J/g·°C

Q8 — Kettle electrical energy with efficiency

Question: How much electrical energy is required to heat 1.5 L of water from 5°C to 100°C if the kettle operates at 78% efficiency?

m = 1.5 kg C = 4180 J/kg·°C ΔT = 95 °C efficiency = 0.78 Q = m C ΔT = (1.5)(4180)(95) = 595,650 J E = Q/eff = 595,650/0.78 = 7.64 × 10^5 J
Answer: E ≈ 7.64 × 105 J

Q9 — Nuclear fission heating water

¹₀n + ²³⁵₉₂U → ⁹²₃₆Kr + ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + 3 ¹₀n + 1.95 × 10¹⁰ kJ

A reactor operates at 64% overall efficiency and circulates 50 megalitres of water.

Q9(a) Final temperature after fission of 235 g U-235 (starting at 2°C)

Useful Q = 0.64 × 1.95×10^10 kJ = 1.248×10^10 kJ = 1.248×10^13 J m = 50 megalitres ≈ 5.0×10^7 kg C = 4180 J/kg·°C ΔT = Q/(mC) ≈ 59.7 °C Tf = 2 + 59.7 = 61.7 °C
Answer: Tfinal ≈ 61.7°C

Q9(b) Mass of U-235 needed to raise water to 100°C

ΔT needed = 98 °C Qrequired = mCΔT = (5.0×10^7)(4180)(98) ≈ 2.05×10^13 J 235 g U-235 gives 1.248×10^13 J (useful) mU = (Qrequired / 1.248×10^13) × 235 g ≈ 386 g
Answer: m(U-235) ≈ 386 g

Q9(c) What happens to ΔT if…

ΔT = Q/(mC) i) Double U-235 → Q doubles → ΔT doubles ii) Half the water mass → m halves → ΔT doubles

Q9(d) Why water is effective for transferring large thermal energy

  • Water has a high specific heat capacity, so it can absorb/release lots of energy with only a small temperature change.
  • It can carry large heat loads and supports convection currents, improving heat transfer.
