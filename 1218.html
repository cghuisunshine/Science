Class #1Scientific Notation, Measurements, Units and Conversions

1.1 Number notation

  • Scientific notation is a convenient way of writing really big numbers and really small numbers.
  • Its general form must be given as:
    • A power of 10 statement to state how many steps the decimal point moved.
      • The exponent is positive for large numbers (× 10x).
      • The exponent is negative for small numbers (÷ 10x = × 10−x).
    • Only one number before the decimal point that can’t be a zero.
    • Rounded to the required number of significant figures or to two decimal points.

To go from scientific to decimal notation:

    • A positive sign means move right.

Class #2Work, Energy, Power and Efficiency

2.1 Summary of the key points

  • The Law of Conservation of Energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another.
  • “All energy is the same as mechanical energy, whether it exists in the form of motion or in that of elasticity, or in any other form.” — James C. Maxwell
  • Energy (E) can be defined as the ability to do work (W).
  • We will explore how energy is transformed from one form to another:
    • nuclear energy
    • thermal energy
    • gravitational potential energy
    • kinetic energy
    • mechanical energy
Figure 2.1 – Different forms of energy.

Units and symbols commonly used

  • P = power (watt, W)
  • W = work (joules, J)
  • ΔE = energy gain (joules, J)
  • Δt = time taken (seconds, s)
  • The unit for energy and work is joules (J).

Power (P)

  • Power (P) is the rate at which work is done or the rate at which energy is transferred.
P = W / Δt P = ΔE / Δt
  • The unit for power is watt (W).

Efficiency

  • Efficiency is the ratio of useful work done to total energy transferred.
% efficiency = (useful work done / total energy transferred) × 100% % efficiency = (W / E) × 100%

Energy as the ability to do work (W)

  • Work (W) is done when an object or particle is moved in the direction of an applied force.
work = force × distance W = F × d force = mass × acceleration W = m × a × d
  • Work is measured in joules (J).
  • Energy can be defined as the ability to do work.
  • The change in energy of a system can be given by the amount of work done on a system.
ΔE = W
Figure 2.2 – Work is the product of the distance an object is moved and the component of the force that is in the same direction (W = F × d × cosθ).

2.2.1 Applications of energy and work to power (P)

  • Power (P) can be defined as the rate at which work is done.
Power = work done / time power = W / Δt power = ΔE / Δt
  • Power is measured in watt (W).
  • Appliances with a greater power rating can do more work in the same amount of time.

2.2 Applications of energy and work to efficiency

  • Energy efficiency refers to the amount of useful work done relative to the amount of total energy transferred.
  • Efficiency describes the ratio of useful energy that is being conserved during a transformation process.
  • Energy is used to do work, but some energy is lost due to resistive forces and commonly converted to heat, sound and light.
  • Energy efficiency is almost always less than 100%.
  • Efficiency can be given by:
% efficiency = (useful work done / total energy transferred) × 100%

OR

% efficiency = (change in energy / total energy transferred) × 100%
Figure 2.3 – Energy efficiency example (input 300 J; useful output 30 J; wasted 270 J; Efficiency = 30/300 = 0.1).

Class #3Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Summary of key points

  • Nuclear reactions are different from chemical reactions.
    • In chemical reactions there are no changes to the size or number of nuclei, mass is conserved and energy changes are relatively small.
    • In nuclear reactions there are changes to the size and number of nuclei and small changes in mass yields massive amounts of energy.
  • Breaking the strong nuclear binding forces yields vast amounts of wave energy and kinetic energy in the form of high-speed subatomic particles.
  • Fission is a nuclear reaction in which a heavy nucleus is split apart, yielding two or more smaller nuclei, subatomic particles and vast amounts of kinetic and wave energy.
    • The daughter products are often radioactive and present a significant waste disposal problem.
  • Fusion is a nuclear reaction in which two light nuclei combine, yielding a single larger nucleus, subatomic particles and vast amounts of kinetic and wave energy.
    • Fusion is the source of energy in the Sun.
    • Fusion techniques are currently being developed (https://www.nature.com/articles/d43978-022-00171-5).
  • Fission is the source of energy for all nuclear power generation used today.
  • During nuclear reactions, the mass defect is converted to high-energy waves and particles according to:
E = mc²
  • We will later relate the thermal energy yield (ΔH) to:
Q = mcΔT

3.2 Nuclear fission

  • Neutrons have no charge and so they are not deflected by the electrostatic force from a positively charged atomic nucleus.
  • Nuclear fission reactors and weapons use this principle to smash large and heavy atomic nuclei into smaller nuclei to release the energy from the strong nuclear binding forces.
    • A neutron (¹₀n) crashes into an atom of stable uranium-235 to create radioactive uranium-236, which then undergoes radioactive decay (see next class).
    • After several steps, nuclei of krypton and barium are formed, along with the release of 3 neutrons and huge quantities of energy.
    • This nuclear reaction is the technique used in nuclear power and nuclear bombs.
Figure 3.1
¹₀n + ²³⁵₉₂U → ⁹²₃₆Kr + ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + 3 ¹₀n + energy
When a uranium-235 nucleus is split by a neutron, barium-141 and krypton-92 are produced with 3 neutrons that creates a chain reaction if sustained and not controlled.
  • Once the nuclear fission reaction has started, it can keep sustaining itself.
    • The neutrons released in the reaction can then trigger more reactions on other uranium-235 atoms.
    • This chain reaction can quickly get out of control and can be regulated by:
      • Controlling the amount of exposure of uranium rods in the reactor of a nuclear powerplant.
      • Using materials that could absorb some neutrons emitted.
    • Nuclear reactors have complex systems to ensure the chain reaction stays at safe levels. An uncontrolled chain reaction can result in the release of destructive energy and harmful radiation. It is on this concept that nuclear bombs are created.

3.3 Nuclear fusion

  • Fusion is the combination of two small atomic nuclei into a single larger nucleus that involves the release of vast amounts of energy.
    • The process requires tremendous heat and pressure.
    • Recently and for the first time, a controlled nuclear fusion reaction produced more energy than the energy used by the lasers that initiated the reaction.
      • The target absorbed 2.05 megajoules of energy and emitted 3.15 megajoules to yield a 54% energy gain.
      • https://www.nature.com/articles/d43978-022-00171-5
    • Fusion reactions are also used in nuclear weapons such as hydrogen bombs.
  • In the core of the Sun, two hydrogen nuclei combine to form a helium nucleus.
    • As a helium atom, a.k.a. an alpha particle (⁴₂He), is formed, vast amounts of nuclear energy is released as waves and as the subatomic particles gain kinetic energy.
Figure 3.2
²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + energy
When a hydrogen-2 nucleus fuses with hydrogen-3 it yields helium-4 and a neutron with vast amounts of nuclear energy.

Class #4Rutherford’s Three Types of Radiation

4.1 Atomic theory and searching for the invisible rays

  • Radiation is everywhere but can be difficult to detect.
  • A brief history on the discovery of radiation:
    • Roentgen coined the term X-rays in 1895 to describe this previously unknown energy that behaved mysteriously.
    • Becquerel realized uranium emitted seemingly invisible energy as well.
    • Marie Curie and her husband Pierre named this energy radioactivity.
      • Early discoveries of radiation relied on photographic equipment and harmful levels of exposure.
      • Later, more sophisticated devices such as the Geiger–Müller counter were developed to measure radioactivity more precisely.
  • Natural background radiation exists all around us in the form of high energy particles or waves being emitted from a variety of materials.
    • Being exposed to radioactive waves and materials is mostly harmful because high energy particles and waves cause damage to DNA and our tissues.
      • UV rays
      • X-rays
      • radiation therapy
Figure 4.1 – Wave energy increases with frequency from left to right in the electromagnetic spectrum.

4.2 Radioactive decay

  • Unstable nuclei continue to emit subatomic particles and waves until they reach a stable configuration.
    • This phenomenon is called radioactive decay.
    • When atoms emit high energy particles and waves, ions and even new atoms are formed with completely new atomic nuclei.
    • Radioactivity results from having an unstable nucleus.
    • Radioactive decay releases energy as radiation.
  • Only certain isotopes of some elements are radioactive.
    • These are called radioisotopes.
    • Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but with a different number of neutrons.
    • The rate of radioactive decay of different radioisotopes have been accurately determined and can be extrapolated to predict the age of rocks and fossils.
  • High energy waves and particles are called radiation when they leave the atom.
    • We will learn about three types of radiation:
      • alpha particles (α)
      • beta particles (β)
      • gamma particles (γ)

4.3 Three types of radiation

  • Rutherford identified three types of radiation by passing radiation through an electric field where charged particles are deflected.
    • Positive alpha particles (α) were attracted to the negative plate.
    • Negative beta particles (β) were attracted to the positive plate.
    • Neutral gamma particles (γ) were not attracted by any plate.
Figure 4.2 – Deflection of positive alpha particles (α), negative beta particles (β) and uncharged gamma waves (γ) when passed through an electric field.

4.3.1 Alpha radiation (⁴₂He or ⁴₂α)

  • Alpha radiation emits alpha particles (⁴₂He or ⁴₂α).
    • Positively charged.
    • Largest and heaviest of the radiation particles.
  • Alpha particles are essentially a helium nucleus and it carries a nett charge of +2.
    • 2 protons
    • 2 neutrons
  • The release of alpha particles is called alpha decay.
  • A sheet of paper can stop an alpha particle.
    • Alpha particles are heavy and slow and have a much lower penetration potential than the other forms of radiation.
Figure 4.2 – The α-decay of radium-226 to radon-222.
²²⁶₈₈Ra → ²²²₈₆Rn + ⁴₂α

4.3.2 Beta radiation (⁰−1β)

  • Beta radiation emits beta particles (⁰−1β).
  • Beta particles are essentially electrons with zero mass and carries a nett charge of −1.
  • Beta decay occurs when a neutron changes into a proton and an electron:
    • proton remains in the nucleus.
    • electron is emitted.
  • A thin sheet of aluminum foil can stop a beta particle.
Figure 4.2 – The β-decay of iodine-131 to xenon-131.
¹³¹₅₃I → ¹³¹₅₄Xe + ⁰−1β

4.3.3 Gamma radiation (⁰₀γ)

  • Gamma radiation emits gamma particles (⁰₀γ).
  • Gamma rays are high energy, short-wavelength radiation with no charge and no mass.
    • Highest energy form of electromagnetic radiation.
  • Gamma decay results from wave energy being released from an unstable, high-energy nucleus.
    • Often, other kinds of radioactive decay will also release gamma radiation.
    • Uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay and also releases gamma rays.
  • It takes thick blocks of lead or concrete to stop gamma rays.
Figure 4.3 – High energy γ-radiation from carbon-12.
¹²₆C → ¹²₆C + γ
Figure 4.4 – Relative penetrating power of alpha (α), beta (β) and gamma radiation (γ).

4.4 Nuclear equations for radioactive decay

  • Nuclear equations are used to show changes in the nucleus of radioisotopes.
  • Rules for writing equations for nuclear reactions:
    1. The sum of the mass numbers is conserved.
      • The sum of large numbers must be equal on both sides.
    2. The sum of the charges (given by atomic number) is conserved.
      • The sum of small numbers must be equal on both sides.

Class #5Half-Life (λ) and Radiometric Dating

5.1 Isotopes and mass number

  • Isotopes are atoms of the same element, with different mass numbers due to a different number of neutrons (n0) in the nucleus.
    • Mass number refers to the sum of protons and neutrons.
    • Isotopes have the same atomic number (p+), but with a different number of neutrons (n0), isotopes have different mass numbers (p+ + n0).
  • Isotopes of an element have the same symbol and same atomic number.
  • Relative atomic mass (AR) = proportional average of the mass numbers for different isotopes of an element.

Example – Boron

  • Consider the relative atomic mass of boron = 10.8
  • Given: 19.9% of boron atoms have 5 protons + 5 neutrons and 80.1% have 5 protons + 6 neutrons.
  • ∴ 19.9% have a mass number of 10, and 80.1% have a mass number of 11.
(19.9/100 × 10) + (80.1/100 × 11) = 10.8

5.1.1 Representing isotopes

  • Isotopes are written using standard atomic notation:
    • chemical symbol
    • atomic number (Z)
    • mass number (A)

5.2 Rates of radioactive decay and half-life (λ)

  • Half-life (λ) is a measure of the time taken for half the original amount of parent radioisotope to decay to a stable daughter isotope.
    • Parent isotope refers to the original radioisotope.
    • Daughter isotope refers to the product formed from radioactive decay.
  • Half-life can be accurately determined for different radioisotopes and it remains consistent and reliable.
    • Specific isotopes have specific and constant rates of radioactive decay.
    • Therefore λ is considered to be constant.
    • Strontium-90 has a half-life of 29 years:
      • 10 g of strontium-90 today (λ = 0) will be 5 g in 29 years (λ = 1) and 2.5 g in 58 years (λ = 2).
mfinal = minitial / 2n, where n = number of half-lives (λ)
